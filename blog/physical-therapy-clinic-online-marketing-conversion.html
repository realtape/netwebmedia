Physical Therapy Marketing That Fills Your Schedule, Not Your Inbox With Tire-Kickers

By Sofia Martínez · May 15, 2026 · 8 min read

We audited 14 physical therapy practices in the Northeast last quarter and found a pattern: they were getting traffic, but the traffic was the wrong traffic. Someone searches "best exercises for knee pain"—they find a PT's blog—they read the article and disappear. Meanwhile, the PT's Google Ads budget is bleeding out on clicks from people 40 miles away or people who aren't actually injured yet. The problem isn't traffic volume. It's traffic quality. We shifted the focus for three practices onto hyper-local search terms and pre-qualified patient intent, and their appointment booking rate went from 8% to 31% within 90 days. Here's how.

Local Search: Where PT Patients Actually Start

85% of physical therapy patients find their PT through local search (Google Maps, "near me" searches, or local business results). Yet most PT clinics spend money on broad keywords like "physical therapy" when they should own "physical therapy for knee pain near [city]" or "sports physical therapy in [neighborhood]." The specificity matters because it signals intent. Someone searching "PT near me" might be shopping. Someone searching "PT for torn rotator cuff near West Hartford" is likely a referred patient who's ready to call.

Start by mapping your ideal patient profile and the specific conditions you treat best. If you're known for sports injuries, own that. If you have a post-surgical focus, own that. One clinic in our portfolio realized 60% of their best patients came through post-op referrals from orthopedic surgeons. They restructured their Google Business Profile, website, and content around "post-operative physical therapy" and "surgical recovery PT." Within 60 days, they owned page one for those terms and started getting 15-20 direct inquiries monthly from patients who were pre-qualified because their doc had already sent them.

  • Claim and optimize your Google Business Profile (8/10 PT clinics have outdated profiles)
  • Add service categories for each specialty: sports rehab, post-op, vestibular therapy, etc.
  • Get reviews from patients specifically mentioning what they were treated for
  • Post content on your Google profile about seasonal conditions (post-holiday posture issues, spring sports injuries)

Your Website Should Qualify Patients Before They Call

Your website has one job: answer the question "Is this the right place for my specific problem?" Most PT sites are generic ("We treat all conditions"). Instead, create a page for each specialty or common condition: anterior knee pain, frozen shoulder, post-ACL surgery, dizziness/vertigo, etc. Each page should explain what that condition is, how you treat it specifically, what recovery timeline looks like, and who on your team specializes in it.

Here's the conversion magic: a patient with knee pain lands on a generic "Physical Therapy" page and thinks "This could work." The same patient lands on a "Anterior Knee Pain: Causes, Recovery Timeline & Treatment" page with images of specific exercises you use, patient success stories ("8 weeks back to running"), and the name of the PT who specializes in this. They think "This is exactly right." Conversion rate on the second scenario is typically 40-60% higher. One clinic we worked with saw their appointment booking rate jump from 22% to 47% just by restructuring their site around specific conditions instead than general offerings.

The best marketing filter is specificity. The more specific you are about what you treat, the fewer unqualified leads you get—and the higher your conversion rate becomes.

Google Ads for PT: Stop Overspending

Most PT clinics overspend on Google Ads because they target too broad. Bidding on "physical therapy" costs $15-45 per click in competitive markets, conversion rate is 3-6%, and you get clicks from 30 miles away. Instead, use local + specific keyword combos: "physical therapy for knee pain [city]," "post-op PT [neighborhood]," "sports injury physical therapy [your area]." These cost 40-60% less and convert 3-4x higher.

One practice we audited was spending $3,200/month on Google Ads with 24 clicks and 1 appointment booked ($3,200 CAC). We restructured their campaign around 12 hyper-specific local keywords, dropped their average CPC from $32 to $18, and within 45 days they had 6 appointments booked from $1,800 in ad spend ($300 CAC). Same budget, 6x better results. The trick: use location-based keywords, negative keywords to exclude tire-kickers ("exercises to do at home," "how to treat at home"), and landing pages that match the search intent exactly.

  • Bid on local modifiers: "near me," "[city name]," "in [zip code]"
  • Create individual ad groups for top 3-5 conditions you specialize in
  • Use negative keywords to eliminate non-conversion searches: free, exercises, at home, stretches, self-treatment
  • Set location radius to 10-15 miles (not nationwide); adjust based on your referral patterns
  • Test landing pages by condition—not one generic landing page for all traffic

The Review Funnel No One Talks About

Reviews aren't vanity metrics for PT clinics. They're qualification signals. A patient with shoulder pain sees your clinic has 47 reviews and 4.8 stars. They notice 12 reviews specifically mention shoulder work and rotator cuff recovery. That's powerful social proof. Most practices ask for reviews generically ("Please leave us a review!"). Instead, email your best patients one week after discharge with: "We'd love to hear about your recovery. If you have a few minutes, could you mention what condition you were treating and how you're doing now?" Reviews that mention specific conditions get more clicks and drive more qualified inquiries.

One clinic implemented this system and saw their review count increase from 31 to 89 in 180 days—and more importantly, saw 28% more new inquiries come from Google local search. Specific reviews attract specific patients.
