Restaurant Delivery Optimization: 5 Metrics That Actually Increase Order Volume

By Sofia Martínez · May 31, 2026 · 6 min read

Restaurant delivery is a margins game that most owners are losing. DoorDash, Uber Eats, and Grubhub take 15–30% per order. On a $15 order, you're left with $10.50–$12.75 in revenue after platform fees. Add packaging, labor, and ingredients, and your net margin on delivery orders is 8–12%—half your dine-in margin. So why do it? Because restaurants that optimize their delivery performance see 2.3x higher order frequency from repeat customers and can negotiate lower commission rates (we've seen successful restaurants get down to 12–15%). The catch: you have to optimize for the metrics that matter.

Metric 1: Delivery Acceptance Rate (Target: 95%+)

When a delivery order comes in, your staff has 30–45 seconds to accept it before the platform reassigns it. Restaurants with acceptance rates below 85% get deprioritized in the platform algorithm. Your store appears lower in customer search results. Fewer orders get routed to you. Acceptance rate is directly tied to how fast you can prepare food and whether your staff is monitoring the system.

A 120-seat casual Italian restaurant in Chicago was stuck at 78% acceptance rate. Their average delivery order preparation time was 18 minutes. Why? One person was handling both dine-in and delivery orders. We advised them to assign one prep staff member exclusively to delivery orders during peak hours (11 AM–1 PM, 5 PM–8 PM). Acceptance rate jumped to 96% within two weeks. Delivery order frequency increased by 31% in month two because the platform algorithm was prioritizing them. One person's cost was offset by the additional order volume within 30 days.

Metric 2: Estimated vs. Actual Delivery Time (Target: Within 2 Minutes)

You promise a customer their order arrives in 35 minutes. If it arrives in 42 minutes, the customer is annoyed and less likely to reorder. If it arrives in 28 minutes, the customer is thrilled and will order again. The platforms track this metric (on-time % and average delay), and restaurants with consistently late deliveries get buried in search results and offered fewer orders.

Most restaurants inflate their estimated delivery time to be safe. A Thai restaurant we worked with estimated 42 minutes when their actual average was 28 minutes. They reduced the estimate to 32 minutes. On-time percentage went from 67% to 89%. But here's the hidden win: because their actual delivery time (door-to-customer) is handled by the platform's driver, the issue was always food prep time. Once we reduced the estimate, they could reduce actual prep time by front-loading orders during slower moments. This freed labor during peak delivery windows, increased staff availability for dine-in, and reduced stress. Order frequency increased 18% because they were finally showing up in 'fastest delivery' filters on DoorDash.

Metric 3: Order Accuracy & Ratings (Target: 98%+ Accuracy)

  • Implement a photo-verification system: Before an order leaves the kitchen, take a photo of the complete order and check it against the delivery slip. Cost: 60 seconds per order.
  • Create a delivery order QA checklist: Run through the checklist before bagging—all items present, correct substitutions applied, special requests noted, sauce and utensils included.
  • Train one staff member as the 'delivery audit' person: Single point of quality control during peak hours. Their only job is to catch errors before they leave.
  • Incentivize accuracy: Track error rate by shift. If a shift hits 99% accuracy for a month, give that crew a $25 bonus pool. It works.
  • Monitor ratings religiously: Check DoorDash, Uber, Grubhub ratings weekly. If your rating drops below 4.6, stop everything and identify the error source (wrong orders, cold food, missing items). Track it back to the kitchen or to driver delays.
A single wrong order costs you $15–$25 in revenue. But it costs you way more in platform algorithm priority. One wrong order per week is burning you 5–8% of potential delivery orders per month.

Metric 4: Repeat Order Rate (Target: 35%+ of Customers Reorder Within 30 Days)

Delivery platforms love stickiness. Restaurants where customers reorder within a month get more consistent order volume and better pricing. One major issue: most restaurants treat delivery customers as faceless transactions. Smart restaurants use the data to build relationships. Implement an email or SMS capture at checkout (99% of platforms allow it). Segment customers by order frequency and order history.

A BBQ restaurant in Texas captured emails for 82% of delivery customers over 6 weeks. They sent weekly emails featuring limited-time menu items paired with images of the dish and a 10% loyalty discount for email subscribers only. Repeat order rate increased from 18% to 41% within 60 days. Incremental revenue from that email list: $2,100/month. Cost of sending emails and managing the list: $40/month. The email capture also created data they could use to negotiate better commission rates with the platforms ('Look, 41% of my customers reorder—your platform is getting reliable repeat volume from us').

Metric 5: Optimal Menu Size for Delivery (Target: 18–24 Items)

Restaurants with massive menus struggle with delivery because more items = more complexity = more errors. We've analyzed 300+ restaurants, and the ones optimizing delivery reduce their menu to 18–24 items offered on the delivery platform only. This is different from dine-in. Your dine-in menu can be 80 items. Your delivery menu should be your 18–24 highest-margin, fastest-to-prepare items.

An Indian restaurant in NYC had 67 items on DoorDash. Prep time was 22 minutes average, accuracy was 71%, and they were barely making margin because they were wasting labor managing so many SKUs. We advised them to cut it to 22 items—their top sellers by margin and prep speed. Average prep time dropped to 14 minutes. Accuracy jumped to 94%. Same revenue ($18K/month), 60% less complexity. They freed up so much labor that they could add a second delivery-only shift. Revenue is now $26K/month from the same restaurant.
